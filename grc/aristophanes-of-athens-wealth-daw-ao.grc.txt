Wealth is a comedy by the Athenian playwright Aristophanes, first produced in 388 BCE. It stands as the last of his eleven surviving plays and marks a significant transition in the evolution of the genre, blending elements of the politically engaged Old Comedy with the more socially oriented themes characteristic of the emerging Middle Comedy. Written in Attic Greek verse, the plot centers on the elderly Athenian Chremylos, who, acting on a cryptic oracle, resolves to seek out the blind god Wealth. The central conceit is that the god’s blindness causes him to distribute riches indiscriminately, often enriching the wicked while the virtuous remain poor. Chremylos, with the aid of his slave Cario, captures the god and has his sight miraculously restored at the sanctuary of Asclepius. This act triggers a comedic revolution in the social order: the just are suddenly rewarded with prosperity, while former misers and corrupt figures are ruined. The play concludes with the triumphant installation of the now clear-sighted Wealth in the Athenian treasury, promising a new era of equitable abundance. The transmitted text is largely complete, structured in 1395 lines with a traditional framework of prologue, choral songs, and episodic scenes.

Composed in the aftermath of Athens’ defeat in the Peloponnesian War, during a period of fragile social and economic recovery, the play reflects broader contemporary anxieties. Its satire shifts from the specific political invective of Aristophanes’ earlier works to a more generalized critique of morality and economic injustice. A notable philosophical interlude is provided by the personified character of Poverty, who vigorously debates Chremylos, arguing that necessity is the true driver of human virtue, industry, and civic stability, thereby presenting a counterpoint to the play’s apparent utopian solution. The text of Wealth has been preserved through medieval Byzantine manuscripts and is regarded as a crucial work for tracing the development of ancient comic theater, with its themes and structure influencing later European dramatic traditions.
