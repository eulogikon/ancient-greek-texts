Argument and Interlinear Glosses-the Persians
Ἐπιχείρημα καὶ Ἐνδιάμεσοι Γλῶσσαι εἰς Πέρσας
by Aeschylus Scholia
eul_wid: tro-aa    eul_aid: tro

Period   Late Antique
Dialect  Byzantine Greek
Domain   Grammar
Format   Verse

The Argument and Interlinear Glosses on the Persians is an anonymous compilation of ancient and Byzantine scholia on Aeschylus's tragedy. This typical scholastic work combines a prose summary of the play's plot with a dense series of explanatory notes placed between the lines of verse and in the margins of the manuscript. Its 981 entries constitute a line-by-line commentary that synthesizes earlier exegetical material from Alexandrian and later grammatical traditions, assembled to aid readers in navigating the playwright's complex and archaic language. The content of the scholia is primarily philological, focusing on the glossing of rare or poetic vocabulary, noting variant textual readings, and parsing difficult syntax and meter. It also provides historical and mythological exegesis, clarifying references to Persian figures and customs, such as the explanatory note distinguishing the three Persian kings named Darius. The scholia to Aeschylus are transmitted primarily through medieval manuscripts, with the most important witness being the 10th-century Medicean codex, which preserves the oldest complete text of the plays alongside this marginal commentary. This specific compilation exists within that broader scribal tradition, having been copied, abbreviated, and conflated over centuries by Byzantine scholars. As a repository of ancient learning, these notes are indispensable for the modern editorial and interpretive study of Aeschylus, offering critical insights into ancient reading practices, preserving otherwise lost textual variants, and illuminating the pedagogical methods used for classical texts in late antiquity and the Byzantine era.
