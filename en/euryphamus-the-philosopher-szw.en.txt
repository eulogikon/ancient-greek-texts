Euryphamus was a late antique Pythagorean philosopher. His surviving treatise is fragmentary. Free to read and download from the Greek library archive.

Euryphamus was a philosopher who likely lived during the 2nd or 3rd century CE, a period of late antiquity marked by the revival and synthesis of earlier Greek philosophical traditions. He is identified with the Pythagorean school, which emphasized mathematics, harmony, and the immortality of the soul. No details of his personal life or origins are known.

He is credited with a single work, On the Soul, which has not survived intact. Our knowledge of it derives from one fragment preserved by the later Neoplatonist philosopher Iamblichus in his own work of the same title. This fragment was later copied into an anthology by Stobaeus in the 5th century. It discusses the soul's nature and its relationship to the body using characteristically Pythagorean concepts involving numbers and harmony.

Euryphamus is an obscure figure, and his significance lies entirely in being a representative of the late Pythagorean tradition. The preservation of his fragment by an influential thinker like Iamblichus indicates that his ideas were considered a valid part of the Pythagorean teachings being collected and studied at the time. This single fragment helps illustrate how Pythagorean themes concerning the soul remained active within the broader Platonic philosophical currents of the Roman imperial era.
